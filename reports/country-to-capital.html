Country to Capital

7:18 Finish time
2nd Age category
Top 10 Female
−5°C Conditions

I'd wanted to do this race for a long time. There's something about a point-to-point that appeals — you start somewhere, you end somewhere else, and the distance in between is the whole point. Wendover to Little Venice, trail then towpath, 70k in January. I was ready.

The first half: trail

I stuck with a pack for navigation through the trail section, which the race director had specifically advised against. I'd do it again. I was checking every turn on my watch anyway, and we were accurate the whole way. No errors, not a single wrong turn. For someone who considers herself rubbish at trail running, I'll take that.

Around mile 10, my nose started bleeding and didn't stop for about an hour. There's not much to say about that. It was annoying. I kept running. Eventually it stopped.

The conditions underfoot were relentless for a naive road runner like me— ice, then mud, then ice again, cycling through for most of the first section. Cold pockets in the low parts of the trail. By the time I hit the towpath I was already tired in ways that didn't fully register yet. But the views were absolutely worthy.

The second half: towpath

Started the towpath section well, hitting around 5:45 pace. Walking a bit after each checkpoint while eating my banana, which was the plan. It was working.

Then, with about 15k to go, something shifted. The thoughts started quietly and then got loud. You can't do this. Why are you so slow on a flat section? I've done enough races to know dark patches exist, but I've never had it hit like this — not this specific flavour of it. The kind that feels like a verdict, not just a bad mile.

Watching sub-7 slip away didn't help. I was exhausted and profoundly lonely out there. The towpath is busy in one sense — cyclists, dogs, joggers — but none of them are with you, and that distance between you and everyone else, when you're already falling apart, is its own kind of brutal.

The last 6k I put a song on repeat and ran slowly (Running up that Hill). The floating towpath sections made me dizzy so I walked those. Physically I could keep moving. Mentally I was somewhere else, and I really needed someone to just be there. That part was hard in a way I hadn't expected and hadn't trained for.

But I kept moving, and I finished.

What actually went well

Wins

Nutrition. Gels, PF gummies, jelly beans, banana at every checkpoint, water and electrolytes throughout. Zero stomach issues. The best I've ever fuelled in a race or training run. It finally clicked — and for someone with celiac disease and milk intolerance navigating race nutrition, getting through 70k with no gut drama is not a small thing.

Navigation. Perfect execution. No errors. I can't believe it either.

The start. Being at that start line for a race I'd wanted to do for ages felt genuinely good.

What hurt

Damage report

Tibialis and ankle stabilisers got properly destroyed in the trail section. I hate shoes with structure. Calves: very tight. Core: strange DOMS-like pain around the ribs — never had that before. No cramps, quads walked away fine. The cold was deep: freezing wind on the second half, cold pockets throughout the first.

After the race

On my way home, a man deliberately tripped me. When I was on the ground he pretended to help me up and was trying to touch me. I didn't eat properly for a while after that. I don't think it's complicated why.

Then came what felt like a sugar crash — shaking hands, cold, nauseous, heart rate spiking. Got some sugar in and felt better after about 30 minutes, but nauseous for longer. Couldn't eat properly until the next day. Probably a combination of the not-eating and post-ultra physiology: blood sugar regulation is genuinely fragile after 70k, and the stress of what happened didn't help.

What I'm taking away

I can nail the practical stuff. Pacing, nutrition, navigation — even in January, on ice and mud, in -5°C with a nosebleed. That's real. What I don't have yet are the tools for when it gets dark in the final stages. The physical and logistical execution was good. The psychological work when exhausted and alone — that's what I'm taking into the next one.

Sub-7 was there. That's encouraging and frustrating in equal measure, but mostly encouraging. I finished 2nd in my age category, top 10 female overall.

Country to Capital, I'll be back.
