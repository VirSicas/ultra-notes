Epping Forest 50K

4:43 Finish time
50k Distance
1st Category
Top 10 Female

The week leading into this one was not ideal. Bad sleep throughout, a completely unexpected work crisis landing on Thursday, and a full day sitting at a desk coding on Friday — which is its own particular kind of damage to the hips before a 50k. I arrived at the start line carrying all of that, plus the knowledge that I hadn't run on a trail since December. Nine or ten months. I know.

The start: nerves, then company

I was pretty nervous on the morning. One of the runners from my new team made sure I didn't go alone, and the journey there included a good chat with a veteran ultra runner — exactly the kind of conversation that settles you down without trying to. I'm grateful for that.

My plan was simple: start slow, get into the race, and try to finish strong. Given how little trail I'd done in the buildup — I'd been relying on hill training to compensate, which, in hindsight, was optimistic — I told myself the goal was just to get around.

The negative thoughts arrived almost immediately. Standing at the start, heading into the first checkpoint, they were just there. I found my team mate early on and stayed close, talking, trying to calm my own head down.

Finding a rhythm

I didn't stop at the first checkpoint — still fully stocked, no reason to. I pushed on, still anxious, and then found two runners with a pace and a vibe I liked. I decided to stick with them.

One was doing a 50k for the first time. The other was a seasoned ultra runner who knew this race well. We chatted. We walked the hills. I took on more nutrition. And slowly, somewhere in that middle section, the anxiety loosened its grip and I started to actually enjoy myself.

The caffeine kicked in properly and I felt genuinely good. I left the checkpoint ahead of them, moving well. The section around the 25k mark — watching runners come back towards you, throwing high fives, knowing you're heading back — was one of those moments that reminds you why you sign up for these things. Time disappeared.

The third checkpoint and Simon

At the third checkpoint I had two cups of electrolyte, which wasn't enough, and a memorable conversation with Simon about, among other things, poo. Race priorities.

My stomach felt oddly full but I was still thirsty, and I knew I needed another gel and didn't take one. That combination — overfull gut, under-fuelled muscles, no more trail legs — started to show. I took two wrong turns. Not the race's fault; I was tired and lost in my own head and genuinely couldn't interpret what my watch was telling me. The second time I dragged some nearby runners the wrong way with me, which I still feel guilty about. Both times, Simon found me and corrected the route. That man's sense of orientation may have saved my race.

The last 7k

The last 7k were very hard mentally. My calves had nothing left — not because I'm weak in that area generally, but because trail running uses them in a completely different way and mine hadn't seen proper trail since the previous winter. I had to stop, walk, and give myself an actual out-loud pep talk at one point.

I switched my watch to countdown mode and ground it out to the finish.

Wins

Thing that worked. The race itself — beautiful route, fully stocked checkpoints, well-marked, and genuinely the friendliest atmosphere. I'd recommend it to anyone without hesitation and I'll be back.

Another thing. The social running in the middle section: finding people with the right pace and vibe, walking hills together, chatting. That's what pulled me out of a very anxious first half and probably saved the whole race.

What didn't work

Nutrition still my weak point. Stomach full but thirsty, skipped a gel I knew I needed. The pattern from previous races: I know what I should do and then don't quite do it at the critical moment.

Trail preparation hill training is not the same as trail running, even if it overlaps. My calves were genuinely destroyed by the terrain by the end. I'd ignored the advice about getting out to somewhere like Greenwich park in the buildup because I assumed the hill work would transfer. It doesn't, not fully.

The shoes barely 10 miles in them at the start. With my very wide feet I have to be careful with anything that isn't an Altra, and these had an uncomfortable upper that I noticed from early on. Never race in almost-new shoes. I know this. I did it anyway.

I'll be back for this one. With trail legs next time.
